\section{The Landscape of Mobile-Assisted Language Learning (MALL)} A critical review of current platforms (Duolingo, Memrise, LingQ).

Digital language learning platforms (LLPs) have become dominant in foreign language education, with over 70 applications serving millions of users through Mobile-Assisted Language Learning (MALL)\cite{karasimos2022battle}. The global market size for these platforms was valued at USD 5.8 billion in 2025, growing further to USD 21.1 billion by 2034 at an estimated CAGR of 15.43\% from 2025 to 2034 by Duolingo commanding over 50\% of global downloads in 2023 \cite{businessresearch2024languageapps}. This rapid expansion has been further driven with the advent of Large Language Models (LLMs), enhancing personalization and the generation of rich learning content. While it took 12 years to create 100 language courses for Duolingo with the use of AI they added 148 new courses in early 2025\cite{duolingo2025launch148}.

    
\section{Identifying Limitations}

However there are significant gaps persist in current language learning platforms\footnote{in this chapter when we refer to current language learning platforms we mean the five applications that Karasimos has examined in his work\cite{karasimos2022battle} with criteria depicted in Chapter 3.2.}:

\subsection{Uniform content}
    All platforms present uniform content regardless of user demographics (children, Gen Z, adults, elderly), interests, or sociocultural perspectives, as well as the cultural differences inherent in languages other than English.\\
    With over 500 million learners, Duolingo is using Machine Learning algorithms to provide learning material, only for the difficulty level \cite{duolingo2025approach}.
    
\subsection{Insufficient multimedia}
    Duolingo content is limited to text and audio based on translation. Hence it is not utilizing the image memory recall
    
\subsection{Lack of authentic, real-world content}
Most language learning platforms—Duolingo, Busuu, Memrise, and Rosetta Stone—lack authentic, real-world materials in their instructional design. LingQ partially integrates such content; however, as noted by Karasimos \cite{karasimos2022battle}, it is not recommended for learning a new language.
As Gilmore in 2007 concludes that authentic material are crucial for developing comunitative competence and to boost learners motivation\cite{Gilmore_2007}. However it is undeniable that authentic language materials are filled with a range of features that are present in authentic improvised conversations that creates difficulties for students' comprehension due to a lack of certain cultural background\cite{LiuDong2024AuthenticLanguageModelsClassrooms}. In this proposed application, authenticity will be central to content design: users will be exposed to genuine audiovisual materials contextualized with cultural explanations and adaptive scaffolding, aiming to combine \textbf{linguistic acquisition} with \textbf{cultural competence}.
